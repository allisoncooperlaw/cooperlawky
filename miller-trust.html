Miller Trust (Qualified Income Trust)

A Miller Trust is a tool that lets someone whose income is over the Medicaid limit still qualify for long-term-care Medicaid — so a parent or spouse can get the care they need without the whole family scrambling.

A Miller Trust solves an income problem — not an asset problem.

This is the whole point, and it’s where most confusion starts. Two families can both be told “you make too much for Medicaid” and need two completely different tools. Find yourself below.

If your problem is income

“My monthly income is over the limit.”

Your Social Security, pension, and other monthly income add up to more than Medicaid allows — even though you clearly can’t afford the cost of care on your own. The income is the only thing standing in the way.

→ You need a Miller Trust (Qualified Income Trust).

If your problem is assets

“I have too much in savings or property.”

The issue isn’t your monthly check — it’s the house, the savings, the CDs, the land. That’s a resource problem, and a Miller Trust won’t fix it.

→ You likely need a Medicaid Asset Protection Trust — a different, irrevocable tool with a 5-year lookback, so timing matters.

≈ $2,982 / month

In 2026, the long-term-care Medicaid income limit for an individual is approximately $2,982 per month. Because this limit is set at the federal level (it’s 300% of the SSI benefit rate), it’s the same in Kentucky, Indiana & Ohio. It changes every year, so treat it as a current estimate, not a fixed rule. If your monthly income is above the limit — even by a little — a Miller Trust may be exactly what lets you qualify.

How it actually works

What a Miller Trust does — and what it doesn’t.

In plain English: your income over the cap gets deposited into an irrevocable trust each month and held in a separate account. Because that money now flows through the trust, it no longer counts against your Medicaid eligibility.

What it does

  • Redirects the income over the cap into an irrevocable trust so it no longer counts against Medicaid eligibility
  • Is funded monthly — the excess income is deposited each month
  • Is held in a separate, dedicated account set up for the trust
  • Lets someone who otherwise qualifies actually get approved for long-term-care Medicaid

What it does not do

  • Does not protect your assets
  • Does not avoid or lower the asset / resource limit
  • Is only needed in income-cap states — Kentucky, Indiana & Ohio
  • Is not the right tool if your real issue is savings or property — that’s a Medicaid Asset Protection Trust
Who needs one

Is a Miller Trust right for you or your client?

A Miller Trust is for someone applying for nursing-home or home-and-community-based-waiver Medicaid whose monthly income exceeds the limit — but who otherwise qualifies. If income is the only barrier standing between a person and the care they need, this is very often the missing piece. Your Medicaid adviser will usually have already identified income as the barrier; if there’s any question, a quick call confirms the Miller Trust is the right document before the application goes in.

Key facts

Three things to know about how it’s built.

🔒

It’s irrevocable

A Miller Trust is irrevocable by design — that permanence is part of what makes the income “not count” for Medicaid. It’s built for one specific job.

🏦

The state is repaid at death

When the beneficiary passes away, the state Medicaid agency is repaid from what remains in the trust, up to the amount of benefits it paid on that person’s behalf.

⏳

No 5-year lookback

Because a Miller Trust deals with income — not transferred assets — there’s no five-year lookback. That’s a key difference from asset-protection planning.

Common questions

Miller Trust FAQ

What is a Miller Trust?

A Miller Trust — also called a Qualified Income Trust (QIT) — is an irrevocable trust that redirects the income above the Medicaid limit so it no longer counts against long-term-care Medicaid eligibility. It solves an income problem, not an asset problem.

Does a Miller Trust protect my assets or savings?

No. A Miller Trust does not protect assets and does not avoid the asset or resource limit. It’s only needed in income-cap states such as Kentucky, Indiana, and Ohio. If your issue is too much in savings or property, the right tool is a Medicaid Asset Protection Trust, which has a five-year lookback.

Who needs a Miller Trust?

Someone applying for nursing-home or home-and-community-based-waiver Medicaid whose monthly income exceeds the limit but who otherwise qualifies.

Is there a five-year lookback?

No. Because a Miller Trust deals with income rather than transferred assets, there’s no five-year lookback. The state Medicaid agency is repaid at death, up to the amount of benefits it paid.
