How Much Does a Will or Trust Cost in Kentucky in 2026?

Real numbers, not ranges. Here’s what you should expect to pay for estate planning in Kentucky right now — and what you actually get for the money.

Allison Cooper, JD, CPA, CFP® · May 2026 · 🕐 8 min read

Almost every law firm website tells you to “call for pricing.” I think that’s ridiculous. You wouldn’t buy a car without knowing what it costs. You shouldn’t have to schedule a consultation just to find out whether estate planning is in your budget.

So here are real numbers for what estate planning costs in Kentucky in 2026, including what we charge at Cooper Law, what the market average looks like, and where the cheaper and more expensive options land.

The short answer

For most Kentucky families, you should expect to pay somewhere between $300 and $6,000 for an estate plan, depending on what kind of plan you need and who you hire.

Why is there such a huge price range?

Three things drive the difference: complexity, customization, and credentials.

Complexity

A single person with no children, no real estate, and modest savings has a much simpler plan than a couple with three kids, a Kentucky home, a vacation cabin in Indiana, and a small business. The first plan might genuinely be fine at $1,500. The second one needs a trust, careful tax planning, and asset titling guidance.

Customization

An online template asks you 20 questions and spits out a document. That’s fine for some situations. But it can’t ask follow-up questions, flag inconsistencies, or notice that your beneficiary designation on your 401(k) contradicts your will. A real attorney does all of those things.

Credentials

Most estate planning attorneys hold a J.D. (the standard law degree). A smaller number also hold a CPA, a CFP®, or an LL.M. in tax. Those additional credentials usually correlate with higher fees because they let the attorney integrate your legal, tax, and financial picture — which matters more than people realize.

What about online services like LegalZoom or Trust & Will?

Online estate planning services genuinely have a place. For very simple situations — a single person, no real estate, no minor children, modest assets — they can produce a functional will for under $200.

Where they break down is when something needs nuance:

  • A house that needs to be re-titled into a trust
  • A blended family where you want to protect a surviving spouse and children from a prior relationship
  • Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts that contradict the will
  • A child with special needs who can’t inherit directly without losing benefits
  • Out-of-state property
  • Anything tax-driven

I see the fallout from these situations regularly. Someone buys an online trust, never funds it, and the family discovers it during probate — which the trust was supposed to avoid. The cost of fixing it (or living with it) is almost always more than the cost of hiring an attorney would have been.

What about probate? How does that factor in?

This is the part most people don’t think about. The cost of not planning — or of planning badly — usually shows up as probate costs after you die.

In Kentucky, probate typically costs 2–4% of the gross estate, plus a 6–24 month delay. On a $500,000 estate, that’s $10,000–$20,000 in court fees, executor fees, and attorney fees — all paid out of the estate before your beneficiaries receive anything.

A trust-based plan costs $3,800 up front and avoids most of that. The math is straightforward.

What’s NOT included in the price?

Honesty here matters. When I quote a flat fee for an estate planning engagement, here’s what isn’t in it:

  • Trust funding work — deed transfers and real-estate re-titling. Drafting your trust documents is part of the flat fee. Actually transferring property into the trust (preparing and recording new deeds, coordinating with title companies, etc.) is a separate engagement priced based on the specific work involved. I’ll always quote this up front so you can decide whether you want me to handle it or do it yourself with my written instructions.
  • County recording fees for deeds — usually $30–$50, paid to the county clerk on top of any deed work
  • Notary fees if you need a mobile notary — usually $25–$75
  • Future amendments — if your life changes and you need to update the documents in a few years, that’s a separate (usually small) engagement
  • Specialized add-ons like Medicaid planning, special needs trusts, or business succession documents — these have their own pricing

If any of those apply to you, I’ll tell you up front so the total is never a surprise.
