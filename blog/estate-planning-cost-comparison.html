How Much Does Estate Planning Actually Cost?

Attorney fees, online services, and the cost of doing nothing — a straightforward 2026 breakdown.

Allison Cooper, JD, CPA, CFP® · May 2026 · 🕐 8 min read

Estate planning is one of those products where the price varies enormously and almost nobody tells you what to expect. You can spend $100 or you can spend $10,000 — on the same goal, with very different results.

Here’s an honest, plain-English look at what the major options actually cost, what you get for the money, and what the hidden cost of doing nothing looks like.

Tier 1: DIY (writing it yourself)

Best for: almost no one
$0–$50

You can technically write your own will. State law usually requires only that it be signed by you and witnessed by two people. Some states recognize handwritten (“holographic”) wills.

The cost is essentially zero. The risk is that the document doesn’t do what you think it does. Wills are interpreted by courts using specific legal language, and ambiguous wording can get the whole thing thrown out. Common DIY mistakes include:

  • Improper witnessing
  • Vague language that leads to disputes
  • Missing essential clauses (no executor named, no guardian designated)
  • Contradictions with beneficiary designations on retirement accounts
  • Failure to address what happens if a beneficiary predeceases you

When a DIY will fails in court, the cost shows up later — in fees to fix what wasn’t set up properly, or in distributions that don’t match what you intended.

Tier 2: Online services (LegalZoom, Trust & Will, FreeWill, Rocket Lawyer)

Best for: very simple situations
$150–$500 (will), $300–$700 (trust)

Online services walk you through a questionnaire and produce templated documents. The quality has improved significantly over the past decade, and for genuinely simple situations they produce serviceable wills.

What you get: A signed will or trust based on your inputs. Often some form of online portal for storage.

What you don’t get:

  • Anyone reviewing your specific situation
  • Coordination with your beneficiary designations
  • Asset titling help (which is often more important than the document itself)
  • State-specific tax planning
  • Tailoring for blended families, special needs beneficiaries, business ownership, etc.
  • Someone to call when life changes

The fundamental problem with online services is that they can’t flag what they don’t know to ask. The most expensive estate planning mistakes I see in my practice usually started with someone who used an online service for a situation that was more complex than they realized.

Tier 3: Hourly attorney

Best for: people who want personalized work without flat-fee predictability
$1,500–$6,000+ depending on complexity

Attorneys who bill hourly typically charge $200–$500/hour for estate planning work. A simple will-based plan might take 4–8 hours; a complex trust-based plan might take 15–25 hours.

What you get: Real legal customization, professional drafting, and someone who’ll catch issues an online service would miss.

What you don’t get: Cost certainty. Hourly billing creates a weird incentive structure for clients — you’re penalized for asking questions or wanting things explained. I personally don’t love this model, which is why I don’t use it.

Tier 4: Flat-fee attorney

Best for: most people who want a real estate plan
$1,000–$5,000 depending on the plan

A flat-fee attorney quotes a fixed price for the engagement, regardless of how many hours it takes or how many questions you ask. This is the model Cooper Law uses, and it’s the model most newer estate planning firms have adopted.

What you get:

  • Custom-drafted documents for your specific situation
  • A consultation that’s actually a conversation, not a billable hour
  • Help with beneficiary designations and asset titling
  • The ability to call with questions without checking your billable balance
  • A clear, predictable total cost

Tier 5: Big firm / specialized practice

Best for: very high net worth, complex business holdings, or specialized situations
$10,000–$50,000+

If you have a taxable estate (currently over ~$13M per person), own multiple businesses, have significant international assets, or need elaborate generation-skipping trusts — a specialized estate planning practice or trusts & estates department at a large firm makes sense.

For most readers of this article, this tier is overkill. If it applies to you, you probably already know it.

The hidden cost: doing nothing

The most expensive option in estate planning is the one most people pick by default: doing nothing.

If you die without any estate planning in place, your family pays the cost — in time, money, and stress. Here’s what that looks like in real numbers:

Probate fees: 2–4% of the estate

In Kentucky, Indiana, and Ohio, full probate for an estate without a will typically costs 2–4% of the gross estate. On a $500,000 estate, that’s $10,000–$20,000 in court fees, executor fees, and attorney fees, all paid out of the estate before your beneficiaries receive a thing.

Time: 6 months to 2+ years

Probate isn’t fast. The simplest cases close in six months. Complex cases can drag on for years — especially if there are disputes, missing heirs, or property in multiple states. During that time, your assets are frozen.

Public record

Everything that goes through probate becomes public. What you owned, what you owed, who got what — all searchable by anyone with internet access.

The state decides who gets what

If you die without a will, your state’s intestate succession rules apply. These rules vary, but they rarely match what most people would have actually chosen. Stepchildren don’t inherit. Unmarried partners don’t inherit. The distribution to your spouse and biological children follows a formula that may not reflect your actual relationships.

The math, simplified

For a typical couple with a home, here’s the comparison:

  • DIY: $0 up front. Likely $5,000–$20,000+ in cleanup later if mistakes surface.
  • Online service: $300 up front. Usually fine for the simplest cases; potentially expensive elsewhere.
  • Flat-fee trust-based plan (couple, joint trust): $4,800 up front. Saves the family $10,000–$20,000+ in probate later, plus avoids 6–24 months of delay.
  • Doing nothing: $0 up front. $10,000–$20,000+ in probate, plus court-determined distribution.

Estate planning isn’t an expense. It’s an investment in what your family will experience after you’re gone.

So what should you actually do?

An honest decision tree:

  • Single, healthy, under 30, modest assets, no real estate? An online will is probably fine. Revisit when your situation changes.
  • Anyone with minor children? Hire an attorney. Naming a guardian is too important to template.
  • Anyone who owns a home? A trust-based plan from an attorney is almost always worth it.
  • Anyone with a blended family, special needs beneficiary, business, or significant assets? Attorney, no question.
  • Anyone over 60? Attorney — long-term care and Medicaid considerations add complexity that online services don’t handle.

If you’re on the fence, the cost of a consultation is usually $0. Most flat-fee estate planning attorneys (including me) will tell you honestly whether you need their services or whether a simpler option is fine for your situation.
